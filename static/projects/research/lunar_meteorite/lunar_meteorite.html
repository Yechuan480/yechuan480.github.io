Sample and Geological Background

Lunar meteorites are rocks ejected from the Moon by large impacts and subsequently captured by Earth's gravity. These samples provide a unique opportunity to study lunar geology without direct missions to the Moon.

My research focuses on a specific lunar meteorite sample, analyzing its mineral composition and water content to understand the Moon's regolith history. The geological context suggests these rocks may preserve evidence of water-ice interactions.

Microscopic view showing alteration minerals in lunar meteorite

Previous Work

Prior studies have identified water in lunar regolith, attributing it to sources like solar wind implantation or meteoritic impacts. Research by Saal et al. (2008) detected water in volcanic glass beads, suggesting an endogenous lunar source.

Other works, such as those by Liu et al. (2012), used hydrogen isotopes to trace water origins, pointing to a complex interplay of external and internal processes. These findings form the basis for my investigation.

Current Work

My current research involves detailed mineral identification using optical microscopy and Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM). I am focusing on alteration minerals to measure hydrogen isotope ratios with Secondary Ion Mass Spectrometry (SIMS).

Preliminary results indicate variability in water content, suggesting multiple sources. Ongoing efforts aim to refine isotopic data and correlate findings with lunar regolith models.
